SECTION 1: WHAT IS fMRI?
================================================================================

Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) is a non-invasive neuroimaging
technique that measures brain activity by detecting changes in blood flow and
blood oxygenation. It is one of the most powerful tools available for studying
the human brain in action.

Unlike structural MRI which shows only the anatomy of the brain (its shape,
size, and structure), fMRI measures what the brain is DOING — which regions
are active during a task, emotion, memory, decision, or sensory experience.

KEY FACTS:
- Invented in the early 1990s by Seiji Ogawa and colleagues at Bell Labs
- Does not use radiation (unlike PET scans or X-rays)
- Uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves
- Measures activity indirectly through blood flow changes
- Temporal resolution: ~1-4 seconds per full brain volume
- Spatial resolution: ~1-3mm depending on scanner strength
- Typical scanner strength: 1.5T, 3T, or 7T (Tesla)
- Session duration: usually 30 minutes to 2 hours
- Subject must remain completely still during scanning
- Scanner produces loud banging sounds (70-100 dB)

WHAT CAN fMRI DETECT:
- Which brain regions activate during specific tasks
- Emotion states (fear, joy, sadness, anger, disgust etc.)
- Cognitive states (attention, memory, decision-making)
- Language processing (reading, speaking, listening)
- Social cognition (face recognition, empathy, theory of mind)
- Pain processing
- Motor planning and movement
- Visual and auditory processing
- Sleep states
- Consciousness levels (useful for coma patients)

CLINICAL USES:
- Pre-surgical brain mapping (finding speech/motor areas before tumor removal)
- Epilepsy focus localization
- Alzheimer's disease monitoring
- Stroke recovery assessment
- Psychiatric disorder research (depression, PTSD, schizophrenia)
- Traumatic brain injury (TBI) evaluation

SECTION 2: HOW fMRI WORKS — THE PHYSICS
================================================================================

fMRI is based on a phenomenon called the hemodynamic response — the brain's
vascular response to neural activity.

STEP BY STEP — HOW THE BRAIN CREATES AN fMRI SIGNAL:

Step 1 — Neural Activity
When neurons fire (become electrically active), they require energy. This energy
comes from glucose and oxygen. As neurons fire more rapidly, their local demand
for oxygen increases sharply.

Step 2 — Neurovascular Coupling
The brain has a rapid response system: when neurons in a region become active,
blood vessels in that area dilate and blood flow increases dramatically —
delivering more oxygenated blood than the neurons actually need. This
over-delivery is the key to fMRI.

Step 3 — Change in Blood Oxygenation
The extra oxygenated blood floods the active area. This changes the ratio of
oxygenated hemoglobin (oxyhemoglobin) to deoxygenated hemoglobin
(deoxyhemoglobin) in the blood. These two forms of hemoglobin have different
magnetic properties — oxyhemoglobin is diamagnetic (slightly repels magnetic
fields) while deoxyhemoglobin is paramagnetic (slightly attracted to magnetic
fields).

Step 4 — The Magnetic Signal
The MRI scanner detects this difference. When an area becomes active, the flood
of oxygenated blood reduces the amount of paramagnetic deoxyhemoglobin — and
this change is picked up by the scanner as a small change in the MRI signal.

Step 5 — The BOLD Signal
This measurable change in signal is called the Blood Oxygen Level Dependent
(BOLD) signal. It is the raw measurement underlying all fMRI data.

THE HEMODYNAMIC RESPONSE FUNCTION (HRF):
The brain's blood flow response follows a predictable time course:
- 0-2 seconds: Neural activity begins (silent to fMRI)
- 2-4 seconds: BOLD signal begins to rise (initial dip sometimes visible)
- 4-8 seconds: BOLD signal peaks (maximum activation visible)
- 8-12 seconds: BOLD signal gradually falls
- 12-20 seconds: Signal returns to baseline
- 20-30 seconds: Sometimes a slight undershoot before full baseline return

This delay between neural firing and measurable BOLD signal is called
neurovascular coupling lag and is a fundamental limitation of fMRI.

MRI SCANNER MECHANICS:
- A powerful electromagnet (1.5T to 7T) creates a uniform magnetic field
- Hydrogen atoms in brain water molecules align with this field
- Radio frequency (RF) pulses knock these atoms out of alignment
- As they realign they emit signals detected by receiver coils
- The spatial encoding of these signals creates 3D images
- Each 3D image is called a volume
- A typical session acquires hundreds of volumes over time

ECHO PLANAR IMAGING (EPI):
The most common fMRI acquisition method is Echo Planar Imaging (EPI).
- Acquires a full brain volume very quickly (1-4 seconds)
- Trades some spatial resolution for speed
- Particularly sensitive to BOLD signal changes
- Susceptible to distortions near air-tissue interfaces (sinuses, ear canals)

VOXELS:
A voxel is a 3D pixel — the basic unit of fMRI data.
- Typical voxel size: 2x2x2mm to 3x3x3mm
- A single brain volume contains ~50,000 to 200,000 voxels
- Each voxel contains the BOLD signal from thousands of neurons
- fMRI cannot detect single neuron activity — it works at population level

SECTION 3: THE BOLD SIGNAL EXPLAINED
================================================================================

The BOLD (Blood Oxygen Level Dependent) signal is the core measurement of fMRI.
Understanding it deeply is essential for all fMRI analysis and AI applications.

BOLD SIGNAL CHARACTERISTICS:
- Amplitude: very small — typically 1-5% signal change above baseline
- Time course: slow (seconds), not milliseconds like neural spikes
- Indirect: measures blood oxygenation, NOT electrical neural activity directly
- Noisy: contains cardiac noise, respiratory noise, scanner drift, motion
- Relative: measured as change from baseline, not absolute activity

WHAT INCREASES BOLD SIGNAL:
- Increased neural firing rates in a region
- Increased metabolic demand
- Stronger sensory stimulation
- Greater cognitive effort
- Emotional arousal

WHAT DECREASES BOLD SIGNAL:
- Reduced neural activity (inhibition)
- Neural suppression
- Default mode network deactivation during tasks

SIGNAL TO NOISE CHALLENGES:
- Signal change is only 1-5% above background noise
- Head motion contaminates signal (even 1mm movement causes artifacts)
- Cardiac pulsation creates rhythmic noise (~1Hz)
- Breathing creates slow noise (~0.3Hz)
- Scanner thermal noise is always present
- This is why preprocessing and statistical analysis are critical

BOLD AND EMOTION:
For emotion detection specifically, the BOLD signal in key regions like the
amygdala, insula, and prefrontal cortex changes predictably with emotional state:
- Fear: strong amygdala BOLD increase
- Disgust: strong insula BOLD increase
- Joy: ventral striatum and medial PFC increase
- Sadness: subgenual ACC and insula increase
- Anger: amygdala and orbitofrontal cortex increase
- Surprise: activates broadly across attention and salience networks
- Calm: default mode network more active, amygdala reduced

SECTION 4: fMRI VS OTHER BRAIN IMAGING METHODS
================================================================================

COMPARISON TABLE:

Method       | What it measures        | Time res | Space res | Invasive | Cost
-------------|-------------------------|----------|-----------|----------|-------
fMRI         | Blood oxygen (BOLD)     | 1-4 sec  | 1-3 mm    | No       | High
EEG          | Electrical brain waves  | 1 ms     | ~cm       | No       | Low
MEG          | Magnetic brain fields   | 1 ms     | ~mm       | No       | High
PET          | Glucose metabolism      | Minutes  | ~5 mm     | Yes (tracer) | High
SPECT        | Blood flow (tracer)     | Minutes  | ~10 mm    | Yes (tracer) | Med
fNIRS        | Blood oxygenation (optical) | 1 sec | ~1 cm    | No       | Low
ECoG         | Electrical (on cortex)  | 1 ms     | ~mm       | Yes (surgery) | High
Single-unit  | Single neuron spikes    | 1 ms     | Single cell | Yes (electrode) | High

KEY DIFFERENCES:

fMRI vs EEG:
- fMRI: excellent spatial resolution, poor temporal resolution
- EEG: poor spatial resolution, excellent temporal resolution
- Many researchers combine both (EEG-fMRI) to get the best of both
- EEG is much cheaper and can be used outside the lab
- fMRI requires participants to remain still; EEG does not

fMRI vs PET:
- PET uses radioactive tracers injected into blood — fMRI uses no tracers
- PET can measure specific neurotransmitter systems (dopamine, serotonin)
- fMRI measures general neural activity via blood flow
- PET has worse temporal resolution than fMRI
- fMRI is now preferred for most cognitive/emotion research

fMRI vs fNIRS:
- fNIRS (functional near-infrared spectroscopy) uses light to measure BOLD-like signal
- fNIRS is portable, cheap, and works during movement
- fNIRS only measures cortical surface (cannot reach deep brain structures)
- fMRI has far better spatial coverage of the whole brain
- fNIRS is used for baby brain imaging, Alzheimer's patient motorcycling studies

fMRI vs SPECT:
- SPECT also uses a radioactive tracer
- SPECT has lower resolution than fMRI
- SPECT is used clinically for epilepsy, dementia diagnosis
- fMRI preferred for research, SPECT still used clinically

SECTION 5: BRAIN ANATOMY — KEY REGIONS
================================================================================

The human brain contains ~86 billion neurons and roughly the same number of
glial cells. It is organized into hierarchical levels of structure.

MAJOR DIVISIONS:

1. CEREBRAL CORTEX (the outer folded surface)
   - Frontal Lobe: planning, decision-making, personality, motor control
   - Parietal Lobe: spatial awareness, sensory integration, attention
   - Temporal Lobe: memory, language, hearing, face recognition
   - Occipital Lobe: visual processing

2. SUBCORTICAL STRUCTURES (beneath the cortex)
   - Amygdala: emotion, fear, threat detection
   - Hippocampus: memory formation and retrieval
   - Thalamus: sensory relay station
   - Basal Ganglia: reward, habit, motor control
   - Hypothalamus: autonomic regulation, hormones
   - Cerebellum: motor coordination, timing, some cognition

3. BRAINSTEM
   - Midbrain: visual/auditory reflexes, dopamine production
   - Pons: sleep, breathing, facial expression
   - Medulla: heart rate, blood pressure, breathing

CORTICAL LAYERING:
The cortex is approximately 2-4mm thick and contains 6 layers of neurons.
Different layers have different input/output connections:
- Layer 4: receives sensory input from thalamus
- Layer 5: sends output to spinal cord and brainstem
- Layer 6: sends output back to thalamus (feedback loop)

HEMISPHERIC SPECIALIZATION:
- Left hemisphere: language production (Broca's area), language comprehension
  (Wernicke's area), analytical reasoning, sequential processing
- Right hemisphere: spatial processing, facial recognition, emotional prosody,
  holistic reasoning, creativity

The two hemispheres are connected by the corpus callosum — a thick band of
~200 million nerve fibers enabling left-right communication.

LOBES IN DETAIL:

FRONTAL LOBE:
- Primary Motor Cortex (precentral gyrus): controls voluntary movement
- Premotor Cortex: motor planning
- Prefrontal Cortex (PFC): executive functions (see Section 6)
- Broca's Area (left IFG): speech production
- Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC): error detection, conflict monitoring

PARIETAL LOBE:
- Primary Somatosensory Cortex: touch, pain, temperature
- Inferior Parietal Lobule: multisensory integration
- Superior Parietal Lobule: spatial attention and manipulation
- Angular Gyrus: language, number processing

TEMPORAL LOBE:
- Primary Auditory Cortex: sound processing
- Superior Temporal Sulcus (STS): voice, biological motion, social cues
- Fusiform Gyrus: face recognition (fusiform face area, FFA)
- Parahippocampal Gyrus: scene and place recognition
- Hippocampus: memory
- Amygdala: emotion

OCCIPITAL LOBE:
- Primary Visual Cortex (V1): basic visual processing
- V2, V3, V4: color, orientation, form
- MT/V5: motion processing
- Lateral Occipital Complex: object recognition

SECTION 6: BRAIN REGIONS AND THEIR FUNCTIONS
================================================================================

This section provides deep detail on each key brain region relevant to emotion,
intent, psychology, and neural pathway AI research.

--- AMYGDALA ---
Location: Medial temporal lobe (bilateral, one in each hemisphere)
Size: ~1.5-2cm, almond-shaped
Subregions: Basolateral amygdala (BLA), Central amygdala (CeA), Medial amygdala

PRIMARY FUNCTIONS:
- Fear detection and fear conditioning
- Threat assessment — rapidly evaluates if something is dangerous
- Emotional salience — flags emotionally important events for memory
- Anxiety and stress responses
- Social cognition — reading emotional faces
- Decision-making under uncertainty/ambiguity
- Modulation of memory consolidation (emotional events are remembered better)

fMRI FINDINGS IN THE AMYGDALA:
- Responds strongly to fearful faces within 170ms
- Activates to angry, disgusted, and surprised faces
- BOLD signal correlates with the intensity of perceived facial emotion
- Encodes both categorical emotions (fear vs. joy) and continuous dimensions
  (arousal, valence)
- Amygdala-PFC connectivity underlies emotion regulation
- In PTSD: hyperactive amygdala, hypoactive PFC
- In depression: abnormal amygdala reactivity and resting state connectivity
- Bilateral amygdala damage: patients cannot recognize fear from faces
- In autism spectrum disorder (ASD): reduced amygdala response to social stimuli

AMYGDALA CONNECTIVITY:
- Receives input from: thalamus (fast, low-res), cortex (slow, high-res),
  hippocampus, sensory areas
- Sends output to: hypothalamus (autonomic responses), brainstem (freeze/flight),
  PFC (emotional regulation), nucleus accumbens (reward/motivation)

--- PREFRONTAL CORTEX (PFC) ---
Location: Anterior frontal lobe
Subregions: dlPFC, vmPFC, mPFC, OFC, vlPFC, ACC

DORSOLATERAL PFC (dlPFC):
- Working memory
- Cognitive control and flexibility
- Planning and reasoning
- Emotion regulation through cognitive reappraisal
- Suppression of amygdala responses (top-down control)
- Reduced activity in depression

VENTROMEDIAL PFC (vmPFC):
- Emotional valuation — how good or bad something is
- Extinction of fear (suppresses amygdala after threat is gone)
- Self-referential thought
- Integration of emotion and decision-making
- Damage causes: poor decisions despite intact reasoning (Phineas Gage case)

ORBITOFRONTAL CORTEX (OFC):
- Reward value encoding
- Emotional learning and reversal
- Social behavior and inhibition
- Encodes expected outcomes
- Implicated in addiction, OCD, and impulsive behavior

ANTERIOR CINGULATE CORTEX (ACC):
- Dorsal ACC: conflict monitoring, error detection, cognitive control
- Subgenual ACC (sgACC): emotional regulation, sadness, depression
  (most consistently implicated in major depressive disorder)
- Rostral ACC: emotion-cognition interface

MEDIAL PFC (mPFC):
- Default mode network hub
- Self-referential processing ("thinking about yourself")
- Theory of mind (understanding what others think/feel)
- Perspective taking

--- HIPPOCAMPUS ---
Location: Medial temporal lobe, adjacent to amygdala
Shape: Curved, seahorse-like structure (hippocampus = Greek for seahorse)

FUNCTIONS:
- Episodic memory formation (remembering specific events)
- Spatial navigation (place cells and grid cells)
- Pattern completion (recognizing incomplete memories)
- Emotion-memory integration (with amygdala)
- Context encoding — memories are always encoded with context

fMRI FINDINGS:
- Activates strongly during encoding of new memories
- Activates during retrieval of episodic memories
- Hippocampal-prefrontal connectivity underlies working memory
- Represents emotion categories in a map-like structure
- Grid-like codes in entorhinal cortex represent emotional "space" (valence/arousal)
- In Alzheimer's: hippocampus is first structure to show atrophy
- In PTSD: reduced hippocampal volume from chronic stress
- In depression: hippocampal neurogenesis is impaired

--- INSULA ---
Location: Deep within the lateral sulcus (buried under frontal/temporal cortex)
Subregions: Anterior insula, Posterior insula

FUNCTIONS:
- Interoception — sensing your own body states (heartbeat, gut feelings)
- Disgust (primary emotion associated with insula)
- Empathy — feeling others' pain activates the insula
- Pain processing
- Social emotions (guilt, shame, embarrassment)
- Awareness of emotional bodily sensations
- Craving and addiction

fMRI FINDINGS:
- Anterior insula: higher-order emotional integration
- Posterior insula: basic sensory interoception
- Bilateral insula damage: loss of ability to feel disgust, impaired empathy
- Hyperactive in anxiety disorders, PTSD
- Activates during emotional experiences across all modalities

--- ANTERIOR CINGULATE CORTEX (ACC) ---
(see also PFC section above)

The ACC is a critical hub bridging emotional and cognitive processing.
It is shaped like a collar around the corpus callosum.

DORSAL ACC (dACC):
- Conflict monitoring — detects when two responses compete
- Error detection — fires when you make a mistake
- Attention allocation
- Cognitive effort

SUBGENUAL ACC (sgACC, Brodmann Area 25):
- Most consistently implicated in depression
- Overactive in major depressive disorder
- Deep brain stimulation (DBS) of sgACC is a treatment for treatment-resistant
  depression
- Regulates mood, emotional tone
- Projects to brainstem autonomic centers

--- STRIATUM AND BASAL GANGLIA ---
Components: Caudate nucleus, Putamen, Nucleus Accumbens (NAcc), Globus Pallidus

NUCLEUS ACCUMBENS (NAcc):
- Core reward circuit structure
- Activated by: food, sex, money, social reward, music
- Dopamine release here signals reward prediction
- Hypoactive in depression (anhedonia — inability to feel pleasure)
- Hyperactive in addiction

CAUDATE NUCLEUS:
- Habit formation
- Goal-directed action
- Implicated in OCD (caudate overactivity)

PUTAMEN:
- Motor learning
- Procedural memory (learning skills)
- Reward and punishment learning

--- THALAMUS ---
Location: Center of brain, bilateral
Functions: Sensory relay station, consciousness gating, attention regulation

All sensory information (except smell) passes through the thalamus before
reaching the cortex. The thalamus acts as a gatekeeper — during sleep and
anesthesia, thalamic gating shuts down, suppressing conscious awareness.

Relevant nuclei:
- Pulvinar: attention, visual salience, threat detection
- Mediodorsal: prefrontal-emotion connections
- Anterior: memory, limbic connections

--- FUSIFORM GYRUS ---
Location: Ventral temporal lobe (inferior surface)

FUNCTIONS:
- Face recognition (Fusiform Face Area, FFA)
- Word form recognition (Visual Word Form Area, VWFA)
- Object recognition
- Expertise effects (chess experts activate FFA for chess positions)

KEY fMRI FINDING (Haxby 2001):
The Haxby study (img_001 in this dataset) showed that the ventral temporal cortex
has a distributed but partially overlapping code for different objects.
The FFA responds specifically to faces but also responds (less) to other objects.
Odd/even run reliability correlations of r=0.81-0.87 show this is a robust effect.

SECTION 7: NEURAL PATHWAYS — HOW REGIONS CONNECT
================================================================================

The brain is not a collection of isolated regions — it is a network of networks.
Understanding connectivity is as important as understanding individual regions.

MAJOR EMOTION-RELATED PATHWAYS:

1. THE FEAR PATHWAY (LeDoux)
   Thalamus → Amygdala (fast, direct — the "low road")
   Thalamus → Cortex → Amygdala (slow, detailed — the "high road")
   
   The low road: ~12ms thalamus-to-amygdala, rapid threat detection
   The high road: ~100ms, more accurate but slower threat assessment
   
   Amygdala → Hypothalamus → Autonomic nervous system (fight/flight response)
   Amygdala → Periaqueductal gray → Freezing behavior
   Amygdala → Nucleus accumbens → Avoidance motivation

2. THE EMOTION REGULATION PATHWAY
   PFC (dlPFC/vmPFC) → Amygdala (inhibitory top-down control)
   
   When you regulate an emotion (reappraise, suppress):
   - dlPFC and vlPFC activate strongly
   - Amygdala BOLD signal decreases
   - This PFC→Amygdala inhibition is the neural basis of emotional control
   - This pathway is weakened in PTSD, depression, and anxiety disorders

3. THE REWARD PATHWAY (Mesolimbic/Mesocortical)
   Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA) → Nucleus Accumbens (mesolimbic)
   VTA → Prefrontal Cortex (mesocortical)
   
   Dopamine neurons in VTA fire to:
   - Reward prediction signals (not just reward itself)
   - Positive surprise (better than expected)
   - They STOP firing to negative prediction errors (worse than expected)
   
   This dopamine signaling underlies learning, motivation, and addiction.

4. THE MEMORY-EMOTION PATHWAY
   Amygdala → Hippocampus (emotional memory modulation)
   
   Emotional arousal (amygdala activation) enhances memory encoding in
   the hippocampus. This is why you remember emotional events better than
   neutral ones. Stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline) mediate this effect.
   
   Hippocampus → Prefrontal Cortex (memory retrieval and working memory)

5. THE DEFAULT MODE NETWORK (DMN)
   Key nodes: mPFC, posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), angular gyrus,
   hippocampus, lateral temporal cortex
   
   The DMN is active when you are:
   - Not doing a task (daydreaming, mind-wandering)
   - Thinking about yourself (self-referential thought)
   - Thinking about others (social cognition, theory of mind)
   - Remembering the past or imagining the future
   
   The DMN DEACTIVATES during externally-focused tasks.
   Failure to properly deactivate the DMN is linked to:
   - Depression (rumination)
   - Schizophrenia (confused self/other boundaries)
   - ADHD (inability to suppress mind-wandering)

6. THE SALIENCE NETWORK
   Key nodes: Anterior insula, dACC, amygdala, thalamus
   
   Function: Identifies the most important stimulus in the environment
   and switches brain resources toward it.
   
   Works closely with the executive control network (ECN) and DMN.
   The salience network decides: "Is this important enough to stop what
   I'm doing and pay attention to it?"

7. THE EXECUTIVE CONTROL NETWORK (ECN)
   Key nodes: dlPFC, inferior parietal cortex, dorsal ACC
   
   Function: Top-down cognitive control, working memory, planning,
   task maintenance, inhibition of irrelevant responses.

STRUCTURAL CONNECTIVITY (WHITE MATTER TRACTS):
While fMRI measures functional connectivity, structural connectivity is
carried by white matter tracts — bundles of myelinated axons.

Key tracts:
- Uncinate fasciculus: connects OFC/frontal lobe to temporal lobe (emotion)
- Cingulum bundle: connects ACC to hippocampus and PCC (memory/emotion)
- Superior longitudinal fasciculus: connects frontal and parietal lobes
- Inferior longitudinal fasciculus: connects occipital to temporal lobe (vision)
- Corpus callosum: connects left and right hemispheres

SECTION 8: EMOTION DETECTION WITH fMRI
================================================================================

Detecting emotions from fMRI data is one of the most active areas of
neuroscience and AI research. Here is the complete picture.

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS FOR EMOTION:

1. BASIC EMOTIONS THEORY (Ekman, 1972)
   - 6-7 universal, biologically hardwired emotions
   - Fear, Joy, Sadness, Anger, Disgust, Surprise (+Contempt)
   - Each emotion has: distinct facial expression, physiology, brain region
   - Cross-cultural, even blind individuals show same expressions
   - Most traditional fMRI emotion paradigms use these categories

2. DIMENSIONAL MODEL (Russell, 1980)
   - Emotions exist on two continuous dimensions:
     * Valence: negative (unpleasant) to positive (pleasant)
     * Arousal: calm/low activation to excited/high activation
   - Any emotion can be mapped to a point in this 2D space
   - Fear: high arousal, negative valence
   - Joy: high arousal, positive valence
   - Sadness: low arousal, negative valence
   - Calm: low arousal, positive valence
   - fMRI BOLD signals correlate with both valence and arousal independently

3. CONSTRUCTED EMOTION THEORY (Barrett, 2017)
   - Emotions are not hardwired — they are constructed by the brain
   - Core affect (valence + arousal) + conceptual knowledge = emotion experience
   - Challenges the idea of specific "emotion brain regions"
   - Predicts distributed, context-dependent patterns rather than fixed regions
   - Supported by some fMRI meta-analyses showing large overlap between emotions

TASKS USED TO ELICIT EMOTIONS IN fMRI:
- Viewing emotional images (IAPS: International Affective Picture System)
- Viewing emotional faces (fearful, angry, happy, neutral, sad)
- Watching emotional film clips (joy, sadness, fear, disgust)
- Listening to emotional music or speech
- Reading emotional words or sentences
- Recalling emotional memories
- Anticipating rewards or punishments
- Social rejection/acceptance paradigms

HOW EMOTION IS DETECTED FROM BOLD SIGNALS:

METHOD 1 — REGION OF INTEREST (ROI) ANALYSIS:
- Draw a mask around a specific brain region (e.g., amygdala)
- Average BOLD signal within that region
- Compare signal between emotion conditions
- Simple, hypothesis-driven, low statistical power for subtle effects
- Most common in clinical studies

METHOD 2 — WHOLE BRAIN GLM:
- Model the BOLD time series using General Linear Model
- Each emotion condition is a regressor
- T-maps or F-maps show which voxels significantly differ between conditions
- FDR or FWE correction for multiple comparisons (~200,000 voxels)

METHOD 3 — MVPA (Multivariate Pattern Analysis):
- Train a classifier on distributed patterns of BOLD activation
- The pattern across hundreds/thousands of voxels classifies the emotion
- Much more sensitive than ROI analysis
- Can decode emotions that show similar average activation but different patterns
- Common classifiers: SVM, LDA, logistic regression

METHOD 4 — REPRESENTATIONAL SIMILARITY ANALYSIS (RSA):
- Compute similarity matrix of brain patterns across all emotions
- Compare this brain similarity matrix to theoretical models
- Answers: "Does the brain represent emotions like the basic emotions model
  predicts? Or like the dimensional model?"

METHOD 5 — DYNAMIC FUNCTIONAL CONNECTIVITY (DFC):
- Instead of static connectivity, measures how connections CHANGE over time
- Emotions evolve dynamically — DFC captures this
- Maps which networks are coupled during different emotional states
- Used in NeuroEmo dataset (Indian fMRI emotion recognition)

SECTION 9: THE 8 PRIMARY EMOTIONS — BRAIN MAPPING
================================================================================

--- FEAR ---
Primary regions: Amygdala (bilateral), Insula, dACC, PAG
Supporting regions: Thalamus (pulvinar), Hippocampus, dlPFC

Brain signatures:
- Amygdala: strongest consistent activation across ALL fear studies
- BLA (basolateral amygdala): processes fear learning
- CeA (central amygdala): triggers fear expression (freeze, flight, fight)
- Insula: bodily fear sensations (heart pounding, sweating)
- PAG: freezing behavior, panic
- Hippocampus: contextual fear (fearful in specific places)

Intent states associated: avoidance, escape, freeze, hypervigilance
Arousal: high | Valence: negative
Psychology tags: threat_response, fear_conditioning, anxiety, phobia, PTSD

--- ANGER ---
Primary regions: Amygdala, OFC, Anterior insula, dlPFC
Supporting regions: ACC, Hypothalamus, Striatum

Brain signatures:
- Amygdala: activates to anger-related stimuli and angry faces
- OFC: frustration and reward blocking activates lateral OFC
- Anterior insula: strong anger activation
- ACC: conflict and anger regulation
- Hypothalamus: fight response, aggression

Intent states associated: approach-aggression, confrontation, dominance assertion
Arousal: high | Valence: negative
Psychology tags: aggression, frustration, hostility, threat_display

--- SADNESS ---
Primary regions: Subgenual ACC (sgACC), Insula, Amygdala
Supporting regions: Medial PFC, Hippocampus, Thalamus

Brain signatures:
- sgACC (Brodmann Area 25): most consistently activated in sadness and depression
- Insula: bodily heaviness, fatigue associated with sadness
- Amygdala: responds to sad faces and sad memories
- Medial PFC: rumination, self-focused sad thoughts
- Reduced striatal activity (loss of motivation/pleasure)

Intent states associated: withdrawal, help-seeking, rumination, disengagement
Arousal: low | Valence: negative
Psychology tags: depression, grief, loss, anhedonia, rumination

--- JOY / HAPPINESS ---
Primary regions: Nucleus Accumbens, Ventral Striatum, Medial PFC, OFC
Supporting regions: Cerebellum, Amygdala (positive), Insula

Brain signatures:
- Ventral striatum: reward anticipation and pleasure
- OFC: processing pleasant rewards
- Amygdala: responds to positive stimuli (not just negative)
- Medial PFC: positive self-relevant thoughts
- ACC: positive affect regulation

Intent states associated: approach, sharing, social bonding, exploration
Arousal: medium-high | Valence: positive
Psychology tags: reward, hedonia, positive_affect, gratitude, love

--- DISGUST ---
Primary regions: Anterior Insula (bilateral), Basal Ganglia, OFC
Supporting regions: Amygdala, ACC

Brain signatures:
- Anterior insula: MOST reliably activated by disgust — core disgust region
- Basal Ganglia: disgust-related avoidance behavior
- OFC: moral disgust (violations of social norms)
- The insula is so strongly linked to disgust that insula damage
  significantly impairs the ability to recognize disgusted faces and expressions

Intent states associated: avoidance, rejection, withdrawal, contamination concerns
Arousal: medium | Valence: negative
Psychology tags: disgust_sensitivity, contamination_fear, moral_disgust, OCD

--- SURPRISE ---
Primary regions: Bilateral amygdala, Anterior PFC, Inferior Parietal Cortex
Supporting regions: Bilateral insula, Superior temporal sulcus (STS)

Brain signatures:
- Amygdala: responds to unexpected stimuli regardless of valence
- Anterior PFC: processes novel, unexpected information
- Inferior parietal: reorienting attention to surprising events
- STS: detecting unexpected changes in social signals
- Broadly activates attention and salience networks

Intent states associated: orientation, exploration, attention reallocation
Arousal: high | Valence: neutral (depends on whether surprise is positive or negative)
Psychology tags: novelty_detection, attentional_capture, prediction_error

--- CALM / CONTENTMENT ---
Primary regions: Default Mode Network (mPFC, PCC), vlPFC, Reduced amygdala
Supporting regions: Insula (interoception), Cerebellum

Brain signatures:
- DMN more active during calm states (default mode engagement)
- Amygdala BOLD signal reduced compared to emotional states
- vlPFC: emotion regulation maintaining calm
- Insula: peaceful interoception
- Slow oscillatory activity in frontal regions

Intent states associated: rest, open exploration, receptivity
Arousal: low | Valence: positive
Psychology tags: emotion_regulation, mindfulness, relaxation, parasympathetic

--- ANXIETY ---
Primary regions: Amygdala, Insula, dACC, Bed Nucleus of Stria Terminalis (BNST)
Supporting regions: Hippocampus, dlPFC, Thalamus

Brain signatures:
- BNST: sustained, diffuse anxiety (distinct from phasic fear)
- Amygdala: threat detection component of anxiety
- Insula: bodily anxiety sensations (chest tightness, nausea)
- dACC: worry and rumination
- Hippocampus: contextual anxiety (anxious in specific situations)
- dlPFC: reduced (impaired cognitive control in anxiety)

Intent states associated: avoidance, safety-seeking, hypervigilance, worry
Arousal: medium-high | Valence: negative
Psychology tags: GAD, social_anxiety, panic_disorder, worry, avoidance

================================================================================
SECTION 10: INTENT DETECTION WITH fMRI
================================================================================

Intent detection — understanding what someone is going to do before they do it —
is one of the most exciting frontiers of brain-computer interface and AI research.

WHAT IS NEURAL INTENT ENCODING?

The brain encodes intentions (plans for action) in the prefrontal cortex before
any behavior occurs. These preparatory neural patterns can be decoded from fMRI.

KEY BRAIN REGIONS FOR INTENT:

Pre-Supplementary Motor Area (pre-SMA):
- Prepares voluntary movements before execution
- Activity here predicts when a person will move (Libet experiments)
- Contains "when to act" information

Anterior PFC (aPFC) / Rostral PFC:
- Prospective memory — remembering to do something in the future
- Holds intentions in mind ("I'm planning to do X later")
- Multitasking — maintaining multiple intentions simultaneously

dlPFC:
- Working memory for intentions
- Goal maintenance over time
- Planning complex multi-step actions

CATEGORIES OF INTENT ENCODED IN fMRI:

1. Motor Intent (movement planning)
   - Region: pre-SMA, primary motor cortex, posterior parietal
   - Can be decoded: which finger to move, which direction to move
   - Applications: brain-computer interfaces for paralyzed patients

2. Decision Intent (choosing between options)
   - Region: OFC, vmPFC, dlPFC, striatum
   - Encodes: value of each option, predicted outcome
   - Decision can be "read" from vmPFC/OFC patterns before the choice is reported

3. Attentional Intent (planning to attend to something)
   - Region: Frontal Eye Fields (FEF), Parietal cortex, dlPFC
   - Predicts where a person will look next

4. Social Intent (what you plan to do to/with another person)
   - Region: Temporoparietal Junction (TPJ), mPFC, STS
   - Theory of mind networks track what others plan and intend

5. Goal-Directed Intent (wanting to achieve a goal)
   - Region: OFC (value), Caudate (goal-directed), Putamen (habitual)
   - Distinction: goal-directed vs. habitual behavior maps to different striatal regions

INTENT-EMOTION INTERACTION:
Emotions powerfully modulate intent:
- Fear → avoidance intent (amygdala drives flight response)
- Anger → approach-aggression intent (OFC frustration → approach behavior)
- Desire/craving → approach intent (ventral striatum dopamine)
- Disgust → avoidance intent (insula-driven rejection)

SECTION 11: PSYCHOLOGY CLASSIFICATION WITH fMRI
================================================================================

fMRI allows classification of psychological states, traits, and processes.

COGNITIVE STATES:

Attention:
- Dorsal Attention Network: TPJ, FEF — voluntary, top-down attention
- Ventral Attention Network: right TPJ, IFG — stimulus-driven attention
- Attention to faces activates right-lateralized networks
- ADHD: reduced dorsal attention network function

Working Memory:
- dlPFC: holds information online
- Parietal cortex: spatial working memory component
- Capacity: ~4 items (+/- 1) — "magical number" (Miller, 1956)
- Load-dependent activation: more items = more dlPFC BOLD signal

Long-Term Memory:
- Encoding: hippocampus, parahippocampal cortex, amygdala (emotional)
- Retrieval: hippocampus, prefrontal cortex (monitoring/verification)
- Semantic memory: lateral temporal cortex, angular gyrus
- Episodic memory: medial temporal lobe, right frontal

Language:
- Broca's area (left IFG): speech production, syntactic processing
- Wernicke's area (left STG): language comprehension
- Superior temporal sulcus: voice processing
- Angular gyrus: reading, semantic integration
- Damage to Broca's: Broca's aphasia (telegraphic speech, intact comprehension)
- Damage to Wernicke's: Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but meaningless speech)

PSYCHOLOGICAL TRAIT CLASSIFICATION:

Introversion vs. Extraversion:
- Extroverts: stronger reward network responses to social stimuli
- Introverts: stronger default mode and frontal responses

Neuroticism:
- Higher neuroticism: larger amygdala responses to negative stimuli
- Stronger connectivity between amygdala and dACC
- Associated with anxiety disorders

Empathy:
- Cognitive empathy: mPFC, TPJ (understanding others' thoughts)
- Affective empathy: anterior insula, ACC (feeling others' feelings)
- Mirror neuron system: IFG, premotor cortex

Impulsivity:
- Reduced OFC volume and function
- Reduced dlPFC control
- Stronger ventral striatum responses to immediate rewards
- Reduced ability to delay gratification

================================================================================
SECTION 12: PSYCHIATRIC DISORDERS AND fMRI
================================================================================

--- MAJOR DEPRESSIVE DISORDER (MDD) ---
Key fMRI findings:
- Hyperactive sgACC (subgenual ACC, BA25): overactive resting state activity
- Hypoactive dlPFC: impaired cognitive control, negative bias
- Reduced ventral striatum response to reward (anhedonia)
- Increased amygdala reactivity to negative stimuli
- Abnormal DMN connectivity (cannot stop rumination)
- Hippocampal atrophy with chronic depression
- Treatment (SSRIs, CBT): reduces sgACC activity, increases dlPFC

--- POST-TRAUMATIC STRESS DISORDER (PTSD) ---
Key fMRI findings:
- Hyperactive amygdala: exaggerated fear response
- Hypoactive vmPFC: cannot extinguish fear
- Hypoactive hippocampus: context gating impaired (fear generalizes)
- Hyperactive dACC: heightened conflict and threat monitoring
- Reduced PFC-amygdala connectivity
- Trauma memories activate different neural patterns than normal memories

--- ANXIETY DISORDERS ---
Key fMRI findings:
- Amygdala hyperreactivity (especially to threat cues)
- Insula hyperactivity (bodily anxiety)
- Reduced PFC regulation of amygdala
- Specific phobia: strong visual cortex + amygdala response to phobic objects
- Social anxiety: exaggerated response to negative social evaluation (faces, criticism)

--- SCHIZOPHRENIA ---
Key fMRI findings:
- Reduced activation in dlPFC during working memory tasks
- Abnormal prediction error signaling in striatum
- Disrupted connectivity between frontal and temporal lobes
- Hyperactive dopamine signaling in striatum
- Aberrant salience attribution (salience network dysfunction)
- DMN not properly deactivated during tasks

--- AUTISM SPECTRUM DISORDER (ASD) ---
Key fMRI findings:
- Reduced response to social stimuli in STS, FFA
- Reduced amygdala response to emotional faces
- Atypical connectivity (local over-connectivity, long-range under-connectivity)
- TPJ reduced activation (impaired theory of mind)
- Enhanced visual cortex responses to objects/patterns

--- OBSESSIVE-COMPULSIVE DISORDER (OCD) ---
Key fMRI findings:
- Hyperactive caudate nucleus (basal ganglia)
- Hyperactive orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)
- Reduced lateral PFC function
- OFC-caudate-thalamic loops stuck in "on" position
- SSRIs and CBT reduce OFC-caudate hyperactivity

--- ADDICTION ---
Key fMRI findings:
- Blunted ventral striatum (nucleus accumbens) response to natural rewards
- Exaggerated craving-related activation to drug cues
- Reduced dlPFC control (increased impulsivity)
- Strong insula activation during craving (interoceptive craving signals)
- Disrupted dopamine signaling in reward circuits

SECTION 13: fMRI DATA PREPROCESSING PIPELINE
================================================================================

Raw fMRI data cannot be analyzed directly — it must be preprocessed to remove
noise and prepare it for statistical analysis.

STANDARD PREPROCESSING STEPS:

STEP 1 — DICOM TO NIfTI CONVERSION
Raw scanner data is in DICOM format. This must be converted to NIfTI (.nii.gz)
for analysis. Tool: dcm2niix (free, open-source).

STEP 2 — SLICE TIMING CORRECTION
fMRI volumes are acquired slice by slice (not simultaneously).
Different brain slices are collected at different times within a TR.
This step corrects for this temporal offset so all slices appear simultaneous.
Important for: event-related designs with fast events

STEP 3 — MOTION CORRECTION / REALIGNMENT
Even small head movements (<1mm) contaminate fMRI data.
Algorithm estimates how much the head moved between each volume and mathematically
corrects the data to align all volumes to a reference volume.
Motion parameters (6 DOF: 3 translation, 3 rotation) are saved as confounds.
Participants with >2-3mm of motion are often excluded.

STEP 4 — FIELD MAP CORRECTION (optional)
Magnetic field inhomogeneities cause geometric distortions in EPI images.
Field maps can correct these distortions.

STEP 5 — COREGISTRATION
The functional images are aligned to the participant's own structural T1 image.
This allows accurate localization of functional activation in brain anatomy.

STEP 6 — NORMALIZATION TO STANDARD SPACE
Individual brains differ in size and shape.
Normalization warps each participant's brain to a standard template (MNI space or
Talairach space) so data can be compared across participants.
MNI152: Montreal Neurological Institute template (N=152 brains averaged)

STEP 7 — SPATIAL SMOOTHING
A Gaussian kernel is applied to smooth the data spatially.
- Increases signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
- Accounts for remaining registration errors
- Makes data more normally distributed
- Typical kernel: 5-8mm FWHM (full width at half maximum)
- Trade-off: smoothing reduces spatial resolution

NOTE: For MVPA analysis, minimal smoothing is preferred to preserve
distributed patterns that carry classification-relevant information.

STEP 8 — TEMPORAL FILTERING
- High-pass filter: removes slow scanner drift (typical cutoff: 128s period)
- Low-pass filter: sometimes applied to remove high-frequency noise
- Band-pass filter: combined (used in resting-state fMRI)

STEP 9 — CONFOUND REGRESSION
Remove noise sources from the time series:
- Motion parameters (6 DOF + derivatives = 24 parameters)
- White matter signal (not neural)
- CSF signal (not neural)
- Global signal (controversial in emotion research)
- ICA-based noise components (aCompCor, FIX)

STEP 10 — BRAIN MASKING
Remove non-brain voxels (skull, eyes, air) from analysis.
Reduces computational load and multiple comparison burden.

QUALITY CONTROL:
- Visual inspection of each preprocessing step
- Check motion parameters (should be <2mm translation, <2° rotation)
- Check signal-to-noise ratio (temporal SNR > 100 preferred)
- Check registration quality
- Framewise displacement (FD) and DVARS metrics for motion assessment

TOOLS FOR PREPROCESSING:
- FSL (FEAT, MELODIC): most widely used, free
- SPM12: MATLAB-based, widely used in Europe
- fMRIPrep: automated, standardized, state-of-the-art (recommended)
- AFNI: comprehensive, free
- Nipype: Python-based workflow manager

================================================================================
SECTION 14: ANALYSIS METHODS — GLM AND MVPA
================================================================================

--- GENERAL LINEAR MODEL (GLM) ---

The GLM is the classical statistical approach to fMRI analysis.

CONCEPT:
At each voxel, the BOLD time series is modeled as a linear combination of
predictors (regressors) convolved with the hemodynamic response function (HRF).

Y = X * β + ε

Where:
- Y = BOLD time series at each voxel (measured)
- X = design matrix (task conditions convolved with HRF)
- β = parameter estimates (regression coefficients)
- ε = residual noise

STEPS:
1. Create design matrix with task timing (stimulus onset, duration)
2. Convolve task regressors with canonical HRF
3. Include confound regressors (motion, physiological noise)
4. Fit the model to each voxel's time series
5. Compute contrast maps (e.g., Fear > Rest, or Fear > Happiness)
6. Apply statistical threshold with correction for multiple comparisons

STATISTICAL THRESHOLDING:
- Voxel-level threshold: usually p < 0.001 uncorrected
- Cluster-level correction: FDR (False Discovery Rate) or FWE (Family-Wise Error)
- Permutation testing: non-parametric alternative

SECOND-LEVEL (GROUP) ANALYSIS:
Individual β maps are combined across participants using a second-level GLM
to make inferences about the population level effects.

LIMITATIONS OF GLM:
- Assumes fixed, average response shape (canonical HRF)
- Treats each voxel independently (ignores spatial patterns)
- Cannot detect information encoded in distributed patterns

--- MULTIVARIATE PATTERN ANALYSIS (MVPA) ---

MVPA treats fMRI data as a pattern classification problem.

CONCEPT:
Instead of testing each voxel separately, MVPA uses the pattern ACROSS voxels
to classify cognitive/emotional states.

WORKFLOW:
1. Extract BOLD patterns for each trial/condition (vectors of voxel values)
2. Split data into training and test sets
3. Train a classifier on training data
4. Test classifier on held-out data (cross-validation)
5. Compute accuracy (chance level = 1/N conditions)

COMMON CLASSIFIERS FOR fMRI:
- SVM (Support Vector Machine): most widely used, high accuracy
- LDA (Linear Discriminant Analysis): fast, interpretable
- Logistic Regression: simple, regularizable (L1/L2)
- Random Forest: handles non-linear patterns
- Neural Networks / Deep Learning: most flexible, needs large N

CROSS-VALIDATION STRATEGIES:
- Leave-One-Run-Out (LORO): train on N-1 runs, test on 1 run (standard)
- Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO): train on N-1 subjects, test on 1 (generalization)
- k-fold: general cross-validation

FEATURE SELECTION:
fMRI produces many more features (voxels) than samples (trials).
This requires feature selection or dimensionality reduction:
- ROI masking: only use voxels within a brain region of interest
- Searchlight: move a sphere through the brain, classify at each center
- PCA/ICA: reduce dimensionality before classification
- Univariate feature selection: select top N most variable voxels

DECODING ACCURACY INTERPRETATION:
- Chance level: 50% (binary), 33% (3-class), 25% (4-class) etc.
- Typical decoding accuracy for emotion from amygdala: 60-75%
- For motor intentions: 80-95%
- For visual stimuli: up to 99% in visual cortex

SECTION 15: MACHINE LEARNING FOR fMRI
================================================================================

ML has transformed fMRI analysis. Key approaches:

SUPERVISED LEARNING:
Goal: predict a label (emotion, task, disorder) from brain pattern
Methods: SVM, Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Neural Networks
Input: voxel-level BOLD patterns (high dimensional: ~50,000+ features)
Challenge: curse of dimensionality (more features than samples)

UNSUPERVISED LEARNING:
Goal: discover structure in brain data without labels
Methods: k-means clustering, hierarchical clustering, ICA, PCA, NMF
Use cases:
- ICA: identify independent brain networks (resting state)
- k-means clustering: identify recurring brain states over time
- t-SNE/UMAP: visualize high-dimensional brain patterns in 2D

TRANSFER LEARNING:
Train model on large dataset (e.g., all OpenNeuro data)
Fine-tune on smaller target dataset (e.g., your specific population)
Important because: single fMRI studies are too small for deep learning from scratch

BRAIN DECODING (FORWARD MODEL):
Predict brain responses from stimulus features
e.g., which voxels respond to which aspects of an image?
Used in: visual neuroscience, language neuroscience

BRAIN ENCODING (INVERSE MODEL):
Predict stimulus features or mental state from brain responses
e.g., what emotion is the person feeling from their amygdala pattern?
This is what most clinical/psychological fMRI AI aims to do

DEEP LEARNING SPECIFIC APPROACHES:

CNNs for fMRI:
- 3D CNNs: treat fMRI volume as 3D image
- 2D CNNs: slice-by-slice or time-series approach
- Strong for visual feature extraction

RNNs/LSTMs for fMRI:
- fMRI time series has temporal dependencies
- LSTMs capture temporal dynamics of emotional states
- Useful for naturalistic paradigms (movie watching)

Transformers for fMRI:
- Self-attention over brain regions
- BrainBERT: pre-trained transformer for EEG/neural signals
- MindEye: reconstructs seen images from fMRI

Graph Neural Networks (GNN):
- Brain naturally represented as graph (regions = nodes, connectivity = edges)
- GNNs operate on functional connectivity matrices
- NeuroEmo uses DFC-GCNN (Dynamic Functional Connectivity + GNN)
- Strong for capturing network-level emotion information

SECTION 16: DEEP LEARNING FOR BRAIN DECODING
================================================================================

Recent breakthrough models for fMRI-based brain decoding:

MINDEYE (2023):
- Reconstructs seen images from fMRI data
- Uses diffusion models conditioned on brain activity
- Achieves near-perfect reconstruction of viewed images
- Trained on Natural Scenes Dataset (NSD)

BRAIN DIFFUSION MODEL:
- Generates brain activity patterns from text descriptions
- Enables data augmentation for small fMRI datasets

LARGE-SCALE PRE-TRAINING:
- Train on thousands of fMRI sessions from OpenNeuro
- Learn general brain representations
- Fine-tune for specific tasks (emotion, intent, disorder)

CHALLENGES FOR DEEP LEARNING IN fMRI:
1. Small sample sizes (10-100 participants per study)
2. High dimensionality (millions of voxels across time)
3. High inter-subject variability (every brain is different)
4. Scanner variability (different sites, field strengths)
5. Motion and physiological noise
6. Indirect measurement (BOLD ≠ spikes)

SOLUTIONS:
1. Transfer learning from large pre-trained models
2. Dimensionality reduction (ICA, ROI masking)
3. Hyperalignment: align individual brain patterns to common space
4. Data harmonization: correct for scanner differences
5. Data augmentation: temporal jitter, noise injection

================================================================================
SECTION 17: FUNCTIONAL CONNECTIVITY
================================================================================

Functional connectivity (FC) measures how different brain regions' activities
are correlated over time — whether they work together as a network.

TYPES OF FUNCTIONAL CONNECTIVITY:

Seed-Based Connectivity:
- Choose a seed region (e.g., amygdala)
- Correlate seed time series with every other voxel
- Creates a whole-brain map of regions that co-activate with the seed
- Example: amygdala seeds shows positive FC with insula, ACC, OFC

Independent Component Analysis (ICA):
- Decomposes fMRI data into independent spatial components
- Each component is a brain network active together
- Identifies resting-state networks without a priori hypotheses
- Common networks: DMN, salience, executive control, sensorimotor, visual

Psychophysiological Interaction (PPI):
- How does connectivity CHANGE between conditions?
- e.g., amygdala-PFC connectivity during fear regulation vs. baseline
- Captures task-modulated connectivity

Dynamic Functional Connectivity (DFC):
- Connectivity changes over the scan session
- Uses sliding window or point-process analysis
- Different brain states emerge dynamically
- Emotions are not static — DFC captures their evolution

CONNECTIVITY MATRICES:
- Parcellate brain into N regions (100-400 regions typical)
- Compute N×N correlation matrix (FC matrix)
- This matrix is input for GNNs and graph-based ML
- Used in clinical diagnosis (can classify MDD, schizophrenia from FC matrix)

KEY CONNECTIVITY FINDINGS FOR EMOTION:

Amygdala Connectivity:
- Positive FC with: hippocampus, insula, ACC, mPFC, OFC
- Negative FC with: dlPFC (executive control suppresses amygdala)
- In PTSD: reduced amygdala-vmPFC connectivity (impaired fear extinction)
- In depression: abnormal amygdala-sgACC connectivity

Default Mode Network (DMN) in Emotion:
- Rumination (depression): excessive DMN activation, especially sgACC
- Mindfulness meditation: reduces sgACC-DMN connectivity, reduces rumination
- Positive emotions: DMN engaged in positive self-referential thought

SECTION 18: RESTING STATE fMRI
================================================================================

Resting state fMRI (rs-fMRI) measures brain activity when participants are NOT
doing any specific task — just lying still with eyes open or closed.

WHY RESTING STATE IS VALUABLE:
- No task design needed (simpler to collect)
- Can be done in patients who cannot perform tasks (coma, infants)
- Reveals intrinsic brain organization
- Reproducible across individuals and sites
- Sensitive to psychiatric and neurological disorders

KEY RESTING STATE NETWORKS (RSNs):
1. Default Mode Network (DMN): mPFC, PCC, angular gyrus, hippocampus
2. Salience Network: anterior insula, dACC
3. Executive Control Network: dlPFC, inferior parietal
4. Sensorimotor Network: primary motor and somatosensory cortex
5. Visual Network: occipital cortex
6. Auditory Network: superior temporal gyrus
7. Dorsal Attention Network: FEF, IPS
8. Frontoparietal Network: lateral PFC, parietal

EMOTIONAL IMPLICATIONS OF RESTING STATE:
- Habitual emotional processing is reflected in resting state
- Anxious individuals: hyperconnected amygdala at rest
- Depressed individuals: overconnected sgACC at rest
- Resting state predicts emotional reactivity to upcoming stimuli
- Baseline emotional state (mood) affects resting connectivity

================================================================================
SECTION 19: TASK-BASED fMRI
================================================================================

Task-based fMRI measures brain responses during specific cognitive or
emotional tasks.

EXPERIMENTAL PARADIGMS:

BLOCK DESIGN:
- Long blocks (20-60 seconds) of a single condition
- Alternating with rest blocks
- Example: 30s of fearful faces, 30s rest, repeat
- Advantages: strong, easily detected signal
- Disadvantages: can't separate individual trial responses
- Good for: localizing regions, clinical mapping

EVENT-RELATED DESIGN:
- Brief individual trials (2-4 seconds each)
- Trials from different conditions interleaved randomly
- Example: 2s fearful face, 2s neutral face, 2s angry face, mixed
- Advantages: can separate individual trial responses, model better
- Disadvantages: requires more trials, lower power per condition
- Good for: cognitive studies, individual trial analysis, MVPA

MIXED DESIGNS:
- Combines both block and event-related elements
- Separates sustained (block-level) from transient (event-level) responses

NATURALISTIC PARADIGMS:
- Participants watch movies, listen to music, hear stories
- More ecologically valid (closer to real life)
- Analyzed with inter-subject correlation (ISC) or GLM with continuous annotations
- Datasets: Emo-FilM, Midnight Scan Club

TYPICAL EMOTION TASK EXAMPLE:
- Stimulus: image of a fearful face (3 seconds)
- ISI: 1-2 seconds fixation cross
- 80 trials per session (20 per emotion × 4 emotions)
- Total scan time: ~8-10 minutes per run
- Behavioral response: rate emotional intensity (1-7 scale)

SECTION 20: fMRI EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
================================================================================

BEFORE THE SCAN — PLANNING:
1. Define research question precisely
2. Choose appropriate task paradigm
3. Power analysis: how many participants are needed?
   (Typical fMRI studies need N=20-30 minimum for basic contrasts;
    N=100+ for reliable individual difference effects)
3. Ethics approval (Institutional Review Board / IRB)
4. Informed consent from all participants
5. Pilot test outside scanner first

PARTICIPANT SELECTION:
Standard exclusion criteria:
- Metal implants (pacemakers, cochlear implants, aneurysm clips)
- Severe claustrophobia
- Body weight > scanner limit (~300 lbs)
- Pregnancy
- Recent head injury
- Specific medication (may affect BOLD signal)

SCAN PROTOCOL PARAMETERS (from OpenNeuro ds002675 used in this dataset):
- Scanner: Siemens Skyra 3T
- Sequence: EPI (Echo Planar Imaging)
- TR (Repetition Time): 4 seconds
- TE (Echo Time): 30ms
- Flip Angle: 90°
- Slice Thickness: 2mm
- Number of Slices: 49
- Matrix: 94 × 94
- Receive Coil: 32-channel head coil
- Phase Encoding Direction: posterior-anterior (j-)
- Total Readout Time: 30.2ms

GOOD SCIENTIFIC PRACTICE:
- Pre-register your hypotheses before collecting data
- Use open data formats (BIDS standard)
- Share your data on OpenNeuro after publication
- Report effect sizes not just p-values
- Correct for multiple comparisons rigorously
- Replicate key findings before claiming a discovery

SECTION 21: KEY RESEARCH FINDINGS
================================================================================

FOUNDATIONAL DISCOVERIES:

1. OGAWA ET AL. (1990): Discovered the BOLD signal
   Blood oxygenation affects MRI signal — foundation of fMRI

2. KWONG ET AL. (1992): First human fMRI
   First demonstration of task-related BOLD signal changes in humans

3. LEDOUX (1996): The Emotional Brain
   Two pathways to amygdala — fast thalamic route and slow cortical route
   Fear conditioning occurs in amygdala

4. HAXBY ET AL. (2001): Distributed representation
   Ventral temporal cortex uses distributed, overlapping patterns for object categories
   Faces and houses can be distinguished from pattern across whole region
   Even-odd run reliability r=0.81-0.87 (img_001 in this dataset)

5. DAMASIO (1994): Somatic Marker Hypothesis
   Emotions guide rational decision-making via bodily signals
   vmPFC damage impairs decisions despite intact reasoning

6. PHAN ET AL. (2002): Meta-analysis of emotion fMRI
   55 studies, N=874 participants
   Amygdala: specifically for fear
   Basal ganglia: positive emotions
   Insula: disgust
   ACC: cognitive components of emotion

7. WAGER ET AL. (2013): Neurological Pain Signature (NPS)
   First reliable multivariate brain "signature" that predicts pain intensity
   Template for emotion signature work

8. WAGER AND CHANG (2015): Negative Emotion Signature
   fMRI + machine learning identifies brain signature predicting negative emotion
   intensity in INDIVIDUALS — not just groups
   Signature spans multiple distributed networks

9. BARRET (2017): Constructed Emotion
   Emotions are not discovered in specific brain regions
   They are constructed from core affect + conceptual knowledge

10. ALLEN ET AL. (2022): The "Emotional Homunculus"
    Emotions activate somatotopically organized sensorimotor regions
    Different emotions map to different body part representations

SECTION 22: LIMITATIONS OF fMRI
================================================================================

fMRI is powerful but has important limitations every researcher must know:

TECHNICAL LIMITATIONS:

1. Indirect Measurement
   BOLD signal reflects blood flow, not neural activity directly.
   Neurovascular coupling can be disrupted by disease, aging, drugs, caffeine.

2. Poor Temporal Resolution
   The HRF peaks at ~5 seconds — events closer than ~2 seconds cannot be
   separated temporally. This is 1000x slower than neural firing (milliseconds).

3. Spatial Resolution Limits
   Standard fMRI voxels (2-3mm) contain ~1 million neurons each.
   Cannot detect single neurons or even small neural ensembles.

4. Motion Sensitivity
   Even 0.5mm of head motion can contaminate data.
   Anxiety and discomfort cause motion. Pediatric and clinical populations
   move more, making data quality worse in exactly the populations we most want
   to study.

5. Noise Sources
   Cardiac (heartbeat), respiratory (breathing), scanner drift, thermal noise.
   Signal change is only 1-5% — these noise sources can be comparable in size.

6. Signal Distortion Near Air
   EPI sequences are susceptible to susceptibility artifacts near air-tissue
   interfaces (sinuses, ear canals). This particularly affects:
   - Orbitofrontal cortex (near sinuses)
   - Inferior temporal lobe (near ear canals)
   - Amygdala (near both)

INTERPRETATIONAL LIMITATIONS:

7. Reverse Inference Problem
   Seeing amygdala activation does NOT necessarily mean "fear."
   The amygdala is activated by novelty, social stimuli, negative stimuli,
   positive arousing stimuli, unexpected stimuli, and more.
   Cannot conclude specific emotion from single region activation alone.

8. Ecological Validity
   Lying in a loud, claustrophobic scanner is very different from real life.
   Emotions experienced during fMRI may differ from real-world emotional states.

9. Individual Variability
   Brain anatomy and function vary enormously between individuals.
   Group average results may not apply to any individual.

10. Publication Bias
    Only positive results tend to be published.
    Many failed replications have occurred in emotion neuroscience.
    Use meta-analyses and pre-registered studies for more reliable findings.

PRACTICAL LIMITATIONS:

11. Cost
    fMRI scanners cost $1-3 million to purchase, plus $500-1000/hour to operate.
    This restricts sample sizes and replication.

12. Accessibility
    Most fMRI research uses WEIRD samples:
    Western, Educated, Industrial, Rich, Democratic populations.
    Neuroimaging data from African, South Asian, Southeast Asian populations
    is severely underrepresented. NeuroEmo dataset (Indian participants) is an
    important exception.

13. Static Snapshot
    Standard fMRI captures average activity over a session.
    Emotions are dynamic and evolve. Dynamic FC helps but has its own limitations.

TOP DATASETS FOR fMRI EMOTION AI:

1. OpenNeuro (openneuro.org)
   600+ open datasets, CC0 license
   Filter by: emotion, language, task, resting state

2. NeuroEmo (OpenNeuro ds005700)
   40 participants, Indian population
   5 emotions: calm, afraid, delighted, depressed, excited
   Bollywood movie clips as stimuli
   DFC-GCNN analysis

3. Emo-FilM
   30 participants watching 14 films (2.5 hours)
   50 emotion annotations per moment
   Heart rate, respiration, fMRI
   High temporal resolution annotations

4. OpenNeuro ds002675 (this repository)
   Language network in traumatic brain injury
   16 controls + 8 TBI patients
   Siemens Skyra 3T, language paradigm

5. Natural Scenes Dataset (NSD)
   8 participants, 7T scanner, 5000 images each
   Brain responses to natural images
   Used for image reconstruction from fMRI

6. Human Connectome Project (HCP)
   1,200 participants, 3T + 7T
   Comprehensive cognitive, structural, functional data
   Emotion tasks included

7. Amsterdam Open MRI Collection (AOMIC)
   ~1,000 participants, 3T
   Emotion matching, face perception, working memory, resting state
   CC0 license on OpenNeuro
